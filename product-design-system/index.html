00 · Governing Law

Two artifacts, one arrow.

The brand identity lives in brand-deck/index.html. The product design system lives here. The inheritance arrow points from brand to product, never the reverse. If a product need is not covered, it goes back through the brand section that owns it. Nothing is invented at the point of use. On 01 Jul 2026 the member register was ratified: the Cabin, tiered (register.html). Its premise governs everything below: reading is the luxury.

The ten rules
  1. 01Two artifacts, one arrow. Brand identity in the brand deck. Product DS here. Inheritance is one-directional.
  2. 02Lock what the brand has decided. Color materials, interaction grid, type tokens, wordmark geometry, icon recipe, voice rules. Copied verbatim. Not re-negotiated.
  3. 03The interaction grid is the law. Every color pairing is measured. Forbidden pairs are forbidden in every role, on every surface, in every component. The same law binds the neutrals: anything meant to be read measures 4.5 or better on its ground; on dark grounds reading cream sits at 0.68 alpha or above. Disabled states and the grid's own illustration of failing pairs are the only exemptions.
  4. 04Warm at the surface, rigorous one layer down, calm always. Every component decision checks against this.
  5. 05Gold is the signal, never the control. Gold never appears as a button fill, a button border, any interactive control, or a trim along a card edge. The gold fix is the bar in the label, never larger.
  6. 06The physician in sentences, the instrument in units. Names and prose use the voice face. Values and units use the voice face too, set tabular at weight 500 against 400 prose: the instrument speaks through alignment and weight, not a second font. The audit face is retired from product surfaces; it survives in the wordmark's tag.
  7. 07No em dashes in user-facing prose. House rule, non-negotiable.
  8. 08Reading is the luxury. Anything that slows the read is the opposite of premium. Parse speed decides every contest between beauty and clarity. Sentence case for everything anyone reads, member or physician: the flight deck is dark, not harder to read. Tracked caps are retired from product surfaces.
  9. 09Emphasis is currency. Three tiers, each earned: one elevated card per surface answering "what do I look at first," flat inset fields for data groups, everything else naked and grouped by air. Tiers never nest. If everything is highlighted, nothing is.
  10. 10Air is structure. Space between groups is at least twice the space within them, at every altitude. Under load, content yields before air compresses. Empty is correct.

Register v1 ratified 01 Jul 2026: the Cabin, tiered. Canonical spec and reference surface in register.html; lineage in direction-studies.html and cabin-studies.html. Layers 2 through 6 below predate the register and are recut surface by surface, Your Data first; rules 01 through 10 already govern every recut. Amended 02 Jul 2026: the audit face is retired from product surfaces; values set in the voice face, tabular, weight 500; Space Mono keeps the wordmark's tag. The brand deck's section 06 charter awaits the matching amendment.

Air · the sense of space · register law

Spaciousness is not leftover room; it is the primary grouping instrument, and it is codified. The scale above supplies the units; the laws below decide how they are spent. The test for any surface: cover the hairlines and the fills, and the grouping should still be legible from the air alone.

  1. A1Air carries the grouping. Proximity first, enclosure second, lines last. If a grouping fails without hairlines, fix the space, not the lines.
  2. A2The ratio law. Space between groups is at least twice the space within them, at every altitude: rows within a group, groups within a tier, tiers on a surface. Reference ladder: 9 within rows, 26 above a group head, 20 between tiers, 20 between columns, 44 into a new section. Proposed 02 Jul 2026: the ladder is tokenized (--air-row, --air-group, --air-tier, --air-section), and new reusable components spend vertical air only through the tokens; a ladder that lives only in prose does not transfer. The ratio law binds only where the markup preserves the grouping tree: a flat DOM has no group boundaries for air to attach to, so chunking must survive from data to markup to style.
  3. A3The measure. Reading prose runs 45 to 75 characters; table notes hold near 48. A full-width line of text is a legibility failure, not a layout choice.
  4. A4Under load, content yields, air does not. When a surface grows, quiet groups collapse to one honest line and detail defers to disclosure. Air is never shrunk to fit more in. The dark cockpit is a spacing law: 104 markers stay readable because most of them stay quiet.
  5. A5Empty is correct. A quiet surface is the product working. No filler, no decorative dividers, no cards added to occupy room. One enclosure between the reader and the paper, never more.

Shape · what acts and what holds · register law

Once every element is a rounded rectangle, radius must carry meaning or it carries nothing. The evidence is settled: when buttons resemble labels and cards blend into ground, people slow down and test before they trust. So shape is assigned, not styled. Every rounded rectangle in the product is either an actor or a holder, kept apart by five redundant cues. Any two suffice at a glance; all five are law.

  1. S1The 10/12 line. Radius declares role. At 10 and below, it acts: controls at 8 to 10, signal tiles at 4. At 12 and above, it holds: fields 12, cards 16, panels 18. No element crosses the line in either direction.
  2. S2Ink acts. Filled ink and strong outlines belong to controls alone. A container is never ink-filled and never outlined heavier than border-base. If it is dark or strongly ruled, a finger is invited.
  3. S3Controls are fixed-height and single-line; containers flow. A control is one thing to do: centered or icon-led, one line, 28 to 44 tall. A box is several things to read: left-aligned, content-driven height, mixed type sizes.
  4. S4Interactive text is ink at weight 500. Annotations are slate at 400. A verb in slate is a whisper the member misses; a note in ink at 500 is a false invitation. Color never signals alone: weight travels with it.
  5. S5Holders and controls keep their assigned radius. Surface tiers never nest. A control carries its own radius wherever it sits, while a holder uses the radius for its tier. The shadow stays Tier 1's alone; controls never float.
  6. S6Square is for specimens, not surfaces. Radius 0 is off the scale and never renders on anything the product itself draws. It is reserved for references and instruments: color material chips, the interaction grid, the type specimen, data tables, the code manifest, and the simulated browser chrome. If it is a card, field, control, tile, or chip, it takes a radius from the scale; if it sits square, it must be one of these named specimens.

03 · Layer 2 · Core Components

The building blocks.

Generic components that inherit tokens and follow the brand's structural rules. Each is defined once and renders in both registers via the semantic token contract. Before any component: the highlight contract. Four jobs, four costumes, never exchanged.

How to highlight · the contract

A member should know what is pressable from shape alone, before reading a word. The discriminator is the verb: verbs press, statuses tell, selections sit, groups hold. Every highlight on every surface is one of these four, wearing its own costume.

  1. H1Verbs press. If it carries a verb, it is a control: filled ink, outlined ink, or plain text link. Controls never take signal color and never sit in an inset field.
  2. H2Selection sits in an inset. Where-you-are states, the rail, tabs, segments, take the flat inset field: no outline, weight 500. Outlines belong to controls; an outlined selection is a button costume.
  3. H3Status never wears a button. Signal tiles hold values and states only: small, radius 4, signal-colored per the grid, no verbs inside. Ink fills belong to primary buttons alone.
  4. H4Emphasis is never a costume. Text that matters more gets weight, scale, or a tier, not an enclosure. A box around plain text promises an interaction the surface will not keep.

Pattern contracts · no local translation

Tokens are not enough. A surface must instantiate the correct pattern for the job. These contracts are binding across member and physician product surfaces unless a later component names a stricter variant.

  1. P1Use the semantic icon map. Plan is the list icon. Quality of Life is the trend icon. Your Data is the person icon. Risk is the shield-check icon. Chat is the message icon. Appointments is the calendar icon. Aliases inherit the same icon: Care Plan maps to Plan, Vitality maps to Quality of Life, Patient Data maps to Your Data, Aleron AI maps to Chat, Journal maps to Appointments. Do not invent local glyphs.
  2. P2Health markers render as a marker list. Use section headings, quiet row dividers, a right-side value spine, and reading notes at 45 to 75 characters. Do not convert member or dashboard health markers into a dense four-column EMR table unless the surface is explicitly a specimen table, code manifest, or raw export.
  3. P3Signal is sparse. Hazard gets the ember tile. Advisory gets text gold. Baseline stays slate or disappears. Normal values do not need badges. Never badge every abnormal value just because a value is abnormal. The question is whether the physician or member must act on it now.
  4. P4Groups are made by air first. Use space and section text before using boxed rows, header bands, or table borders. A banded section header is a specimen-table move, not the default product move.
  5. P5Physician does not mean cockpit. Physician surfaces may carry more clinical density, but they still use the same tier grammar, icon map, signal rules, and reading law. If a physician surface feels like an instrument panel before it feels like a document or cabin, it is drifting.

04 · Layer 3 · Domain Components

The Aleron-specific set.

These are the primitives that prevent drift. They encode the brand's interaction model: the instrument speaks underneath, the physician speaks above. Each component is demonstrated live with real data. The rail comes first: it is the one structure every desktop surface repeats.

The rail · the constant left edge

Every desktop surface hangs off the same left rail, so it is codified once. The anatomy is fixed, top to bottom: the lockup, the person, a rule, the tabs. The member set is Plan, Quality of Life, Your Data, Risk, Chat, Appointments, in that order, always. The physician set is Inbox, Panel, Reviews, Messages in the intelligence inbox. One variant only, and it is about whose rail it is: on a member surface the person is the member, at the top, with the accountable physician at the foot; on a physician surface the person is the physician, the user, at the foot. Everything else about the rail is identical across surfaces and registers. Nothing else may live in the rail.

  1. R1The anatomy is fixed. Lockup 18, identity block (avatar 32 circle, name 13 at 500, member number 11 slate), rule, tabs, physician foot. 188px wide on desktop. The rail never scrolls and never collapses below its labels.
  2. R2One selection, one dot, legible in both registers. The active tab is the flat inset field per H2: radius 10, weight 500. It must read as clearly on dark as on light. A recessed inset vanishes on the night ground, so the dark register lifts the selection to a lighter panel (--surface-selected) tuned so its contrast against the ground meets or exceeds the day register's; day recesses, night lifts, both are equally findable. At most one attention dot lives in the rail at a time: 6px, gold, a circle. If two sections want attention, the more urgent takes the dot and the other waits.
  3. R3Items are one line. Icon 16 from the codified set, label 13.5 voice, 9px vertical padding. Labels never wrap, never truncate, never abbreviate; the rail is sized to its longest label.
  4. R4Dark is the same design in a different color space. The identical markup resolves through the night tokens: ground, text, inset, borders swap; nothing else may change. Same anatomy, same order, same icons, same content, same spacing. If a dark surface cannot be produced by the token swap alone, the component is wrong, not the mode.
  5. R5Two navs, two mechanisms, never mixed. The primary rail, the constant left edge of an app surface, is icon plus label with the inset selection above; member and physician surfaces share it exactly. A secondary index, a documentation nav or an in-panel history list, is text only and marks its place with a gold tick and weight, never an inset. A surface picks one mechanism and holds it; the two never appear in the same list.
  6. R6Below the hinge, the rail is replaced, never shrunk. At content widths under --rail-hinge (760px) the rail yields to the bottom bar: the same tabs in the same order, icons at 20, the same inset selection, at most one attention dot. The rail never collapses to icons alone and never squeezes below its labels; under the hinge it does not compress, it hands over. Proposed 17 Jul 2026, provisional.

05 · Layer 4 · Interaction Archetypes

The product is alive.

The layers below this one specify how to render data that already exists. This layer specifies what happens when the product does something: when AI speaks, when a patient dictates, when a value changes live, when the system proposes an action. Seven archetypes, each with its own primitives, states, and forbidden patterns.

The three shifts · why this layer exists

A design system that stops at buttons and cards describes a printed page. Aleron is not a printed page. Three forces define the product experience:

AI proposes and explains
A model suggests a lab order, a care-plan change, a referral. It explains why, with sources. The human disposes. The design system must specify the grammar of that exchange: trust, calibration, escalation, evidence.
Humans speak and listen
Patients describe symptoms aloud better than in writing. Physicians leave handoffs as voice notes. The system transcribes, plays back, and lets both modes coexist in the same field. Voice is a first-class input.
The system is alive
Data changes without reload. Connections sync in the background. Background tasks run. Notifications arrive and leave. The surface breathes, and the design system specifies how it breathes.
Archetype 01 · AI conversation · the ask surface

Two surfaces, not one. An inline popover for pointed questions ("Why is this flagged?"), and a persistent thread for deliberation ("Walk me through this"). A keyboard accelerator (Command+K) routes between them. Every assistant message cites its source. AI chrome stays neutral; gold is reserved for the signal it carries, not the button it sits on.

  5. 05Identify which domain components the spec requires.
  6. 06Identify which interaction archetypes the surface needs (AI, voice, agentic, onboarding, data capture).

Acceptance · the seven tests a surface must pass
  1. T1Cover the words. Shape alone says what presses. Verbs look pressable, statuses do not, selections sit in insets. (H1 to H4.)
  2. T2Squint. Cover the hairlines and the fills; the grouping still reads from air alone, and between-group space is at least twice within-group space. (A1 to A5.) This test has a numeric form and it is the required one: measure rendered text-to-text air on the built page, and between divided by within reads 2.0 or better at every altitude. Source cannot pass this test; only the render can. Tooling: air-audit.js.
  3. T3Flip the register. Change the root data-register value between day and flight-deck; nothing else changes. The legacy .night class remains a compatibility alias. Any color that survives the flip unresolved is a defect. (R4.)
  4. T4Count the golds. At most one gold detail per surface. If a One-Thing card is present, its label bar is the gold; on an instrument, the selection focus is the gold. Never both.
  5. T5Check the floors. Every value carries a unit; reading prose at 14 and up; functional voice text at 10.5 and up; every color pair passes the interaction grid. Instrument exception, provisional 03 Jul 2026: on charts, annotations secondary to a plotted mark may run 9.5 minimum, per the physician register's O1.
  6. T6Empty is correct. Nothing was added to occupy room. Quiet groups collapsed to one honest line. A surface that got busier without new information fails this test.
  7. T7Every string would ship. Extract the rendered copy deck with copy-audit.js and give every string a provenance class per the string law below: a spec'd constant, a variable fed from the data model, or sanctioned AI content wearing its provenance chrome. A string with no class fails, and the tells scan reports zero unwaived flags. Vocabulary alone never convicts; provenance decides.
Copy · the string provenance law · provisional 03 Jul 2026

Production surfaces have no anonymous strings: every string is a designed constant or arrives through a variable. Prototypes are held to the same discipline, because filler copy is how a prototype stops predicting the product. This law extends A5, T6, the lorem rule, and the sourceless-AI rule from elements to the words inside them.

  1. C1Every string has a provenance. A rendered string is one of three things: a constant from the surface's spec vocabulary, a variable fed from the data model, or AI-authored content delivered by a named product feature wearing its provenance chrome. A string that is none of these is filler and does not ship, in prototype or production.
  2. C2The surface never narrates itself. No string describes, explains, or coaches the interface: no "this view shows," no "use this panel to." If a surface needs a caption to be understood, the design failed the parse-speed law; fix the design, not the copy. Spec'd furniture is not narration: a screen's one-line definition and an audit footer state what a thing is, in the product's voice, and are ratified per surface.
  3. C3Data-bearing text comes from the data model. A value, a patient-specific sentence, or a clinical read rendered from a literal in the markup instead of the model is a mock leak; it will read plausible and be wrong. If the prototype needs example prose, the prose lives in the data object where it is visible, auditable, and swappable.
  4. C4The AI never speaks anonymously. Model-authored summaries and reads are a product feature with a name, a source line, and a confidence basis (Archetypes 01 and 02). Any string with an AI-authored register and no chrome is forbidden, which is the copy form of the sourceless-message rule.

Tooling: copy-audit.js beside this file; inventory() extracts the rendered copy deck, tells() flags self-narration, coaching, hedge preambles, slop vocabulary, placeholders, and em dashes, with waivers for spec'd furniture. First field audit of the physician chart: 1,588 rendered strings, 3 filler (one coaching panel), 2 candidates that classified clean as data. Vocabulary collides with clinical language ("comprehensive" is an E/M term), which is why provenance, not word lists, decides.

The ratchet · decisions accumulate

When a surface forces a decision this system does not cover, a new mark, a new furniture rule, a new register clause, the session does not end until the decision is proposed here: named, one rule, where it applies, marked proposed. Proposed rules are followed or amended, never re-invented. This is how the system grows a jurisdiction at a time; the unmodeled-window indigo and the hazard tile entered exactly this way, from the physician chart work of 02 Jul 2026.

Dialects · the retrofit law
  1. D1Native. Built from the registry: DS class names, DS token names, no alias layer. Every new build is native.
  2. D2Dialect. A living prototype that predates the system keeps its own vocabulary and maps DS-canonical values in through an alias table declared in its head comment. A dialect syncs values, not grammar; the drift lives in that gap. Dialects are debt: tolerated, dated, and graduated to native when the surface is next recut.
  3. D3Silent dialects are forbidden. An asset that neither builds from the registry nor declares its alias table is out of contract.

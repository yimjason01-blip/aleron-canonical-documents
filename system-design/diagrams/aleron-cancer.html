The Aleron cancer model

Four site engines · sporadic burden collection · sensitivity, not a single score

Illustrative cancer sensitivity sketch. No cited cancer calculator runs on this page. Tyrer-Cuzick, PLCOm2012, QCancer, and PBCG are reference models only. Displayed percentages use custom, unvalidated demonstration arithmetic. Read the qualitative sensitivity patterns, not the risk values.

No composite. Cancer is not one equation. It is four independent validated site engines, breast (Tyrer-Cuzick/IBIS), lung (PLCOm2012), colorectal (QCancer), and prostate (PBCG), each emitting its own number. They cover four of the top five cancers by US incidence and are the clinically actionable sporadic cancers: a validated model exists and screening changes the outcome. Below them sits the sporadic burden collection, the shared modifiable drivers (tobacco, adiposity, alcohol) for the self-actionable cancers that have no screen (bladder, kidney, uterine, melanoma, average-risk pancreas). It surfaces footprint and action, never a number. The thesis of this page: across these models, sensitivity is concentrated. Move smoking and lung explodes; move almost anything else and risk barely shifts. That flatness is why most sporadic cancer cannot be individually risk-stratified. Sporadic burden: the cancers with no screenself-actionable · no number · behavior is the lever

These are the high-incidence sporadic cancers in the amber slice above. None has a validated screen, so there is no engine and no risk number. What they share is a small set of modifiable drivers, so the only lever is behavior. A row lights when your inputs raise that driver for this patient.

Why most cancer can't be risk-stratifiedliterature-true thesis · illustrative fold figures

The fold range from best to worst inputs in each engine, on a shared scale, independent of the patient above. Lung spans more than 100x because smoking carries a relative risk in the tens, the single largest lever in cancer epidemiology. The others span from single digits up to about 14x; prostate's range is PSA, a genuinely strong test that carries its own overdiagnosis cost. Colorectal has no single big lever, just a stack of small ones. A factor can be a real population cause and still be a weak individual predictor (AUC ~0.55 to 0.65). That is why, outside lung, the value is in doing the screen on schedule and moving the modifiable drivers, not in the precision of a personal number.

Model behaviors to keep honestreal design rules
  • Four numbers, never summed. There is no total cancer risk. Aggregating across sites with different baselines destroys the signal, which is why the v1 global burden index was killed.
  • Prostate is not an incidence risk. PBCG predicts cancer on biopsy, weighted to high-grade. It is a shared-decision aid carrying an overdiagnosis cost, shown here as a biopsy/high-grade probability.
  • The burden footprint has no number by design. The same drivers (tobacco, adiposity, alcohol) light up several sites, but they route to behavior, not to a composite score.
  • Liver and pancreas are not here. Liver is conditional surveillance gated on liver disease; average-risk pancreas is a high-risk flag. Neither yields a general-population number.
  • Illustrative magnitudes. The response shapes are anchored to literature; the absolute percentages are not the validated equations. Read the slopes, not the digits.

Engines referenced: Tyrer-Cuzick/IBIS (breast), PLCOm2012 (lung, Tammemagi 2013), QCancer (colorectal, Hippisley-Cox 2015), PBCG/PCPT (prostate, Ankerst 2018). Burden footprint attributable fractions: Islami 2018 (ACS). The widget's arithmetic is an illustrative sketch and is not validated against these calculators; the qualitative result it illustrates, lung far steeper than the other engines and most sporadic factors weakly predictive, reflects real published epidemiology.
